The Challenge We're Solving

Understanding the limitations of current bird sound identification systems

The Problem

Automated bird sound identification systems play a crucial role in biodiversity monitoring by enabling large-scale analysis of acoustic field data. However, existing models operate under a closed-world assumption‚Äîthey only recognize species in their training sets and often misclassify unfamiliar calls as known species with high confidence.

To overcome this limitation, we propose developing a classification system capable of both accurately identifying known bird species and detecting out-of-distribution sounds that may correspond to rare or previously unrecorded species‚Äîaddressing the critical "What if they are new?" challenge.

Why This Matters

1

Silent Misclassification

Unknown species are labeled as known with false confidence, leading to inaccurate biodiversity assessments and potentially missed discoveries.

2

Discovery Barriers

Rare or new species go undetected in monitoring data, hindering conservation efforts and scientific discovery of potentially endangered species.

3

Poor Transferability

Models trained on specific regions fail when deployed in different geographic areas with different species compositions.

Our Solution

We propose developing a classification system capable of:

‚úì Accurately identifying known bird species from acoustic recordings
‚úì Detecting out-of-distribution sounds that may correspond to rare or previously unrecorded species
‚úì Providing calibrated uncertainty estimates for expert review

Prototype A: Minimal Baseline <10% accuracy ‚ùå
  • ‚Ä¢ 2,000 samples, 5-second window, 128 mel bins

  • Lesson: Short windows miss complete vocalizations

  • ‚Ä¢ 4,000 samples, 5-second window, 256 mel bins

  • Lesson: Need full dataset and band-pass filtering
Final System: Production Ready 77% accuracy ‚úÖ
  • ‚Ä¢ 14,620 samples, 10-second window, 256 mel bins

  • ‚Ä¢ Confidence scores consistently >0.97 for correct predictions
